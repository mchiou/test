Quick Overview

Early stage fundraising occurs in three major phases: incubation phase, seed round, and venture round. Each startup has its unique trajectory through these fundraising phases. Some companies skip incubation and/or the seed round entirely and go straight to the venture round. In the following graphic, we show the funding pathways of some designer founded companies.

Incubation

The incubation phase is the earliest phase of fundraising and its main purpose is to get you off the ground. For the purposes of this guide, we’ve defined the incubation phase as earliest amount raised up to about 500K that is not recognized as an official seed or venture round. The main sources of funding during this phase are friends/family, accelerators, angels, crowdfunding, mechanisms, and yourself.

Seed Round

The seed round follows a period of incubation, usually once there has been some notable traction. This round of funding is more formal that incubation and therefore comes from more limited range of sources, specifically seed funds, angels and VCs. The purpose of this round is to take you to the next level (i.e., “shift gears”) so that you can hit some significant traction. It’s hard to define these phases by monetary amount because every company is different, but for the purposes of this guide, we’ve defined a seed round to be roughly ~500K - ~1 million.

Venture Round

A venture round is more formal and now-a-days in Silicon Valley, about ~1 million and beyond. Investors such as angels, seed funds, and VCs from the seed round often participate as well as new investors come on. VCs tend to play a larger role than previously. The main goals of the venture round is to scale, take the market share, and/or continue to gain significant traction.

Pros

  • Grow and sustain your team
  • Move fast to capture the market share
  • A valuable clarifying process

When to Fundraise

When it comes to fundraising, timing is everything. There’s an optimal point in your team and product’s development when it’s best for you to seek funding. At an early stage, the most ideal time for you to fundraise is when you have a solid team, working prototype and some traction.

At the same time, if you have an awesome team with a great track record, you can get away with not building or launching a prototype during the incubation and seed round because investors will be making a bet on you more than anything else. Such talented teams might skip the incubation round altogether and go straight to a seed or venture round, asking for a significant chunk of money before having built anything. The tenets of lean startup would argue, however, that it’s better to stay “lean” and test out your assumptions before committing heavily to investors. How Much to Fundraise

You’re ready to fundraise - how much should you go after? There is no ideal amount as it varies company to company. Here are some helpful criteria in setting your amount.

  1. List out all expenses with an expected and worse case. Know company costs health insurance, payroll tax, etc.
  2. Add a 30 - 50% buffer to your worse case. This buffer covers unforseen costs.
  3. Align fundraising goals with projected milestones, that way you ask for money at your best moments. Project funding for 18 months.

Who to Fundraise From

Sources of funding range from (1) yourself, (2) friends and family, (3) crowdfunding (4) accelerators, (5) angels, (6) seed funds to (7) VCs. As noted above, different phases involve different funders.

Yourself

Client work or other jobs may be a way to fund your idea or grow your creative side project into a venture, especially at the very early stages. Other work can act as a safety net of income, and reduces the amount of financial risk you’re taking on. Be warned: working multiple jobs can feel like a large task and lead to burnout. This is also known as “bootstrapping,” entrepreneurs do whatever it takes for their startup to survive, which can includes taking out loans, opening up credit cards and dipping into personal savings to pursue their idea.

Also, if you start your venture while working for another company, look to make sure that there is no overlap between your anticipated business and your employer’s business. Be careful of any agreements you may have signed with your employer, for you do not want another employer own your work.

Designer Tip: You have the skills to freelance.

You have design skills that are usually in high-demand. You can have the flexibility of freelance while testing the waters with entrepreneurship.

Friends and Family

Friends and family are easy to access and tend to be more sympathetic to your needs. The flip side is that they may not be accredited investors, and they’re usually less connected than investors and other mechanisms. You also take the risk of mixing personal with business.

Crowdfunding

A “democratic version” of angel and VC funding, a large number of individual stakeholders can invest in your business.

Equity crowdfunding, a new form of funding for startups, is an exciting development which allows entrepreneurs to use online crowdsourcing to accept investments.

Currently, only accredited investors and angels can legally invest through such platforms. The Securities and Exchange Commission (i.e., U.S government) is still adjusting the regulations so that ordinary people can participate in equity crowdfunding.

Successful projects on crowdfunding platforms have to communicate their idea in a beautiful and concise manner, usually with solid images and videos. Kickstarter has a strong mission to specifically help fund creatives.

Accelerators

Accelerators typically give incubation funding and help offset a lot of the early stage infrastructure costs like physical office space. They also provide a community of mentors, experts, fellow startups, and access to angel investors and VCs.

Incubators want to get in early on deals. It’s a cheap way for them to get a bunch of equity, across several early stage companies.

They’ll have a standardized process (i.e., pre-set deals) for giving funding and experience in the problems you’ll be facing in getting off the ground.

Angels

Angel investors are a major source of capital during all phases of fundraising. Some angels were once entrepreneurs, so they understand what you’re going through and want to give back. They’re often aligned with the your incentives and care more about your relationship than huge financial returns. Angels can therefore support an entrepreneur who will take a small exit, whereas VCs are incentivized for larger ones. However, remember that various types of angels, some are familiar with market terms, and others may be new to the startup world.

They can also invest small amounts of money, which is what you need at an early stage, set direct terms, understand the experimental nature of your idea, and make fast investment decisions. While an angel is a general name for pre-institutional money, angels often come in three flavors:

Individuals: These angels are accredited independently wealthy investors, who give you money, in exchange for equity or a convertible note.

Network/groups: They sometimes come together to form networks and groups, to share research and consider deals together. Individual angels within networks still make their own investment decisions.

Funds: Angel funds are groups of investors who pool money to invest together, usually alongside additional investment capital from individual members and limited partners.

Prepare

Put Your Story Together

Your story should include some basic building blocks: characters, conflict, and resolution. It includes your team members, the problem you are trying to solve, and how you plan to solve it. Like in a story, setting is also important as it provides the context and backdrop for all the other components.

Above all, what is your unique position? Known as “positioning” in marketing: How can you position yourself in a unique place in the mind of your audience?

Analogies or one-liners work in this regard, such as making references to established notions: “We’re like Pinterest for your career goals.” In addition to being sticky, they help “de-risk” the idea by linking it to a successful predecessor (such as Pinterest in the former example).

By sheer virtue of being a designer, you are a storyteller. Craft a compelling story that has your audience ( “user”) in mind.

Build Relationships with Investors

Building relationships with investors should start before you begin fundraising, though it does intensify during your fundraising sprint. Being a part of an established community, such as an accelerator, helps enormously and is one of their main advantages.

At an early stage, the best way to find investors is to leverage existing contacts to schedule friendly meetings - which are not as formal as a pitches (though you definitely want to show something visual) but more of a meeting in which you ask for advice.

These “friendlies” are particularly effective with angel investors. Follow-up meetings are then opportunities to show a new piece of functionality to your prototype or product or update them on traction (user growth, other investors who have committed).

It’s a subtle courting in which you aren’t directly asking for money, but updating the investor on your progress. If they’re interested, they’ll most likely let you know.

There’s some truth behind the cliche: “Ask for money and get advice; ask for advice and get money.”

@khoi

“Nobody said, yes, come back when you've got a technical co-founder and I'll give you money. There was none of that. They said, ‘Just keep in touch. Let's keep talking about this.’ That was fine for me.”
Khoi Vinh, Mixel
Tweet

At the same time, after a certain amount of friendlies and follow-ups, a more direct ask (“X investor has agreed to put in, I’m closing the round soon - are you interested?”) might be appropriate.

A successful meeting with one investor can snowball into other meetings and opportunities. With every meeting, always ask if there is someone else they recommend you get in touch with. You have to gage from the interaction whether they’re comfortable doing an introduction. Beware of asking for referrals from an investor who has decided not to invest in you.

Designer Tip: Always Show Something

Even during friendlies and casual networking with non-investors (i.e., mentors and fellow entrepreneurs), having something tangible to show at an early stage is valuable. It gets the person you are talking with to engage with your idea and feel excited to help you out.

Engage

Pitch

In an early stage, pitch decks may have less use when meeting with investors but are helpful for you to know your talking points. When raising a seed round (>500K) from seed funds VCs, a pitch deck can be pretty crucial.

Here’s a possible flow for a pitch deck:

  • Depth/breadth of problem
  • Innovation of your solution
  • Size of market/customer base
  • Traction and validation
  • Strength of team (i.e., why you?)
  • What you need to move forward (the ask)

Make sure to cover all the factors investors evaluate before investing:

  • Team
  • Problem/Opportunity
  • Market
  • Product
  • Traction
  • Distribution
  • Business Model
  • Investors

“Designers are in a great position, because most founders have to rely on English to communicate their idea, and designers can elevate their pitch beyond the language. They can take it to the visual realm, and that is always more impressive than what you can articulate with words.”
Zach Klein, Vimeo

Tailor your pitch deck based on your investor’s focus, related investments, and the time you have.

Part of your pitch may be a demo of your product. Walk through a user’s thoughts and interactions in a compelling use case. Don’t make the demo too long by going through all possible use cases, just hit on the most compelling aspect for your product. The key is to tell a good story that gives investors a taste but keeps them wanting more.

Demos can also be a bit risky in terms of time and technical mishaps. If you plan to do one, just make sure you rehearse and budget time accordingly (and have have a back-up).

As a designer, you have a distinct advantage because of your ability to make and create. Leverage these skills to create a compelling pitch deck and demo.

Negotiate Term Sheets

A term sheet is a document that contains the terms and conditions of a proposed investment. In the earliest funding stages (less than 1 million), the term sheets will address convertible note financing. In these situations, you would typically be the one serving the term sheet, or the finalized documents, to your investor. In these instances, the process can be less structured as you may be dealing with different types of investors with different experiences and expectations.

In the later stages of fundraising, term sheets are handed over by investors, and with many VCs, particularly in the Silicon Valley, the term sheet negotiation process has been standardized. Term sheets are conditional offers, and while they are not legally binding, consider it a done deal. It is rare for reputable investors to back out once a term sheet has been given. At the same time, remember that investors will do more research, or due diligence. Term sheets and due diligence can occur sequentially or in parallel. The terms can change at any time before the deal is closed, and investors may want to renegotiate terms after due diligence findings, so the first round of negotiations generally sets the benchmark the deal. While it’s exciting to get investors on board, negotiate carefully. Certain terms can dilute your equity, so have a good lawyer help you with negotiations, and complete the due diligence process before signing the dotted line.

If you are given a term sheet before business diligence has been performed, remember that VCs typically include “no-shop agreements,” or stipulations that prevent you from looking for other investors. You want to avoid taking yourself off the market and putting yourself in a vulnerable situation before both you and your investor have completed the due diligence process.

A few key areas of negotiation include:

  • Valuation: The value of your company before investment.
  • Liquidation Preferences: In the case that your company is liquidated, which can include acquisition, bankruptcy, and the sale of of a company's assets, liquidation preferences state who gets paid first.
  • Vesting Plans: The process where founders and other common stockholders earn their ownership stake over time (usually 2-3 years). This is an investor’s method of ensuring that founders stick around.
  • Anti-Dilution Protection: If your company goes through more rounds of funding, anti-dilution provisions prevents an investor’s shares from decreasing in value.
  • Board Composition: The board determines the activities of a company. So, who’s on the board, and who keeps control of the board?

There are a number of other areas that are addressed in term sheets. For more comprehensive information on term sheets, great resources include Brad Feld’s Term Sheet series, as well as Founder’s Fund termsheet calculator and termsheet explained.

  • Brad Feld’s Term Sheet Series
  • Founder’s Fund: The Transparent Term Sheet
  • Founder’s Fund: What’s In A Term Sheet?

Due Diligence

Due diligence is the process of thoroughly vetting or understanding the risks of a potential investment. This process goes both ways: investors will perform due diligence on your company and vice versa. The purpose of performing due diligence is so each party knows the relationship they are getting into and there are no subsequent surprises.

Due diligence on your company

An investor will perform two types of due diligence: business diligence and legal diligence. While business diligence can happen in parallel with term sheet negotiations, legal diligence happens after the negotiations.

With business diligence, an investor wants to affirm the financial health and overall business potential of your company. This process often includes:

  1. Evaluation of the team
  2. Validation of the product
  3. Size of the market
  4. Sales and marketing strategy
  5. Competitive advantage

An investor also performs legal diligence to ensure all the business findings are legally supported. This will include documents such as intellectual property agreements, articles of incorporation, ownership information, real estate leases, and more.

To get through the due diligence process:

  1. Get everyone (employees, leadership) on the same page
  2. Ask and prepare for what the investor needs ahead of time
  3. Be open and honest

At the end of the due diligence process, an investor may ask you to clean up problem areas (missing documents, minutes, signatures). After everything is in order to an investor’s satisfaction, the process can move forward.

Due diligence on an investor

Your investors are the ones who you are going to be accountable to at some point and you’ve got to have aligned values.”

While investors are conducting due diligence on your company, it is important for you to do sufficient due diligence on a potential investor. Get to know your investor in a variety of settings. Be sure to talk to their portfolio and check references. Through these interactions, you should get a sense of 1. are they reputable and 2. can you work with them? Be sure you know what relationship you’re getting into.

Investor Due Diligence Checklist

What you should learn through talking to other companies in their portfolio and asking others in the industry.

Money - Do they have the money?
  • Who controls the money?
  • Who makes the decisions?
  • How much money does an investor have, and how much time do they have? Some investment funds have a limited life times. If you want to possibly get money from an investor in the future, you want to get in early, when an investor still has the money and time.
Strategy - How do I fit with their portfolio?
  • Are they long-term investors or are they flippers?
  • What’s their track record?
  • What’s their relationship with other investors?
Personality - Can I see them as my boss?
  • Do they seem excited about your idea?
  • Can they help you when you’re in a tough spot?
  • What’s your personal chemistry?

V. Glossary

Incubation:
Early stage funding from various sources, mainly friends/family, accelerators, angels, crowdfunding - with the purpose of getting a startup off the ground.
Seed round:
A formal “round” of funding from angels and VCs. In Silicon Valley today, this is roughly beyond 500K - or a round before a Series A.
Traction:
Your current success in terms of users and revenue.
Venture round:
A round of funding from VCs (and angels). In Silicon Valley today, this is roughly beyond 1 million, includes Series A and beyond.
“Bootstrapping”:
Funding your startup by being scrappy; without external financial support.
Accelerator:
An organization that provides support to startups in various ways: funding, networks, resources (space etc.).
Equity:
Stock in a privately owned company.
Equity crowdfunding:
A new form of fundraising which uses online crowdsourcing to accept investments from accredited investors.
Non-equity crowdfunding:
A form of fundraising which uses online crowdsourcing to accept investments from anyone - without an exchange of equity.
Convertible note:
A convertible note is a loan, given at an early stage, that converts into equity (at a discounted rate) after the startup raises a priced round (generally Series A because there is more information available to establish a fair price.
Series A:
First serious round of venture funding.
“Friendlies":
Warm-up and networking meetings with investors, in which one does not ask for investment but is warming-up to that.
“Snowball effect”:
Meetings and networking that lead to increased momentum.
Term sheets:
Document that contains the terms and conditions of a proposed investment, usually not legally binding.
Due diligence:
Process of an investor vetting your company and its legal history; process of you vetting an investor.
No-shop agreements:
Stipulations that prevent you from looking for other investors.
Valuation:
The value of your company before investment.
Liquidation Preferences:
In the case that your company is liquidated, which can include acquisition, bankruptcy, and the sale of of a company's assets, liquidation preferences state who gets paid first.
Vesting Plans:
The process where founders and other common stockholders earn their ownership stake over time (usually 2-3 years). This is an investor’s method of ensuring that founders stick around.
Anti-Dilution Protection:
If your company goes through more rounds of funding, anti-dilution provisions prevents an investor’s shares from decreasing in value.
Dividends:
The profit distributed between shareholders. Dividends come in the form of cash or stock, and they are often paid quarterly.
